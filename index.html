Situation

  • IR lists at Queen Elizabeth University Hospital (QEUH) are subjectively becoming increasingly disorganised for a variety of reasons.
  • Patient safety is at risk and staff morale has been negatively affected.
  • Current demand for work exceeds capacity and this proposal does not seek to address this fact at all; this is to improve patient safety and flow in IR for whatever work is performed.

Background

  • Both QEUH IR labs have booked cases every day from Monday to Friday, with the only routine exceptions being certain Friday mornings where only one lab is in use.
  • A list is up to six cases, up to four of which can relate to dialysis access procedures.
  • In the hybrid theatres, up to 60% of work involves IR.
  • This volume of work was not accounted for when planning IR activity was undertaken.
  • Few IR lists have vacant slots.
  • On any given day, new cases of varying urgency and from varying locations may be referred.
  • These referrals should be processed in an efficient and consistent manner while minimising disruption to the current lists.
  • The current informal arrangements for covering unplanned cases has been implicated in at least one Serious Clinical Incident.

Assessment

  • Two consultants and up to two registrars are on site at QEUH to consent patients and perform procedures.
  • In practice, it is rare for any of these doctors to be able to review patients on the wards after a procedure.
  • These doctors often have to deal with referrals, requests for advice or imaging review, both in person in the IR reporting room and over the telephone.
  • There is no single number for clinicians to contact IR, with phones in the IR reporting room, control room and recovery all receiving clinical queries.
  • This can disrupt case planning, reporting and other thought processes relating to procedures about to be performed.
  • Work is often duplicated. Questions are often duplicated. Answers are often different.
  • There are two currently consultants each day to be the 'flexible' IR (except on a Friday morning when there has never been a flexible consultant) one covering AM and one PM.
  • The flexible consultant is available to perform QEUH cases in situation where a third operator may be required or to travel to Glasgow Royal Infirmary (GRI) or Gartnavel General Hospital (GGH) to perform urgent procedures on days on which there is no routine list.
  • This consultant should also be free for the above referrals etc. but in practice they are rarely the first to be involved with these unscheduled events.
  • The nursing team is split between the recovery area and the labs.
  • Because the labs are situated between the IR reporting room and recovery, messages are often passed in a chain through the labs.
  • Secretaries with responsibility for booking cases are not all based on site.

Response

The IR team should be split in to the lab team, whose priority is the patients undergoing scheduled procedures, and the duty team, who have an additional role in streamlining unscheduled care and potentially performing emergency procedures. Duty IR consultant should replace flexible IR.

IR duty team

  • A team of one IR doctor, the nurse in charge of recovery and the lead radiographer for the day should form the IR duty team.
  • The IR doctor should have a specific phone for the role.
  • Ideally, this phone should be portable in that it will work in GGH and GRI as well.
  • At the least it should work in QEUH.

Duty IR doctor

  • Consultant (or 6th year registrar) with no other clinical commitments that day.
  • Note, they should not use this session to schedule any cases specific to their practice.
  • The duty session lasts from 0900-1700.
  • The extra session could be claimed from the consultant's SPA session that week: as the duty day should only occur fortnightly the surrendered SPA can be re-claimed the following week.
  • Duty IR should not take place the day after an on call.
  • The duty IR doctor will be based in QEUH IR reporting room and should have access to a PACS workstation.
  • Exceptions to the above may be to attend an MDT, but duty IR doctor should not be based in their office or another reporting room, as a significant amount of interruptions come from physically present clinicians; it is not the job of the lab IR team to try to locate duty IR in these circumstances or to deal with the clinician themselves.
  • Duty IR should attend the daily brief.
  • Duty IR may have to perform urgent procedures in QEUH but that is not their main role.
  • Duty IR may have to travel to other hospitals to perform urgent procedures if there is no list at that site.

Duty IR nurse and radiographer

  • They should help the duty IR doctor in planning referred cases in terms of ensuring there is a request, finding an appointment etc.
  • If the duty doctor is performing a clinical role (eg. performing an urgent procedure) the nurse or radiographer should take the telephone.
  • In this situation, the nurse or radiographer would not be expected to have clinical discussions, but rather they would be able to inform the referrer that duty IR is unavailable and take an appropriate message.
  • An exception to this would be truly emergency or life-threatening cases, such as haemorrhage, which may need immediate discussion, in which case any IR doctor could be interrupted.
  • While it may seem more appropriate that the telephone is passed to another doctor, ie. who is part of the lab IR team, remember both that the point of this exercise is to protect patients by diverting unnecessary interruptions from the QEUH lab team and also that the majority of calls do not need immediate attention.
  • If the system fell back to its default position at the first hurdle then it would not be robust.

Morning brief and hand-over

  • At the morning brief, the duty IR doctor, recovery nurse and lead radiographer should be identified so everyone knows who should take questions etc.
  • Overnight cases to be handed on should be passed from the on-call consultant to duty IR before the brief, using the duty telephone.
  • Duty IR should hand over 'side-of-the-board' cases to the on call consultant around 1700.

Referrals for procedures

  • All telephone and personal referrals should be directed to duty IR, even if one of the lab IR doctors is currently available.
  • If a referral is taken, duty IR, together with the recovery nurse and radiographer, are responsible for prioritising and scheduling the case, including vetting requests and liaising with secretaries.
  • Specific information relating to procedures - eg. contact details, names of referrers etc. - should be recorded in CRIS as this is accessible to everyone and is easily auditable.
  • The ideal scenario is that for every referral which truly requires a procedure some time soon, the referral is concluded by the duty IR team finding an appropriate appointment for the patient.
  • Scraps of paper and side-of-the-board scribbles should be avoided for most cases.
  • Urgent cases which should be performed at the next available opportunity but which cannot be specifically scheduled, eg. cases requiring CEPOD anaesthetic assistance, can be written on the side-of-the-board as they may need to be handed over (vetting should still be performed.)
  • Similarly, cases referred from outside GG&C can be recorded here, as they will not yet have a CRIS entry.
  • Cases discussed and accepted but awaiting CEPOD anaesthetic support should be consented by the duty IR doctor taking the referral. While this may mean that consent is taken by a doctor not ultimately performing the procedure, it should ensure that all of these cases have valid consent.
  • The nursing staff, radiographers and IRs responsible for the day's list should not be involved in any part of the referral process and should only be informed if a case is added to the day's list.
  • The judgement of the duty IR team should be respected in this regard.
  • Duty IR doctor should try to summarise an added-on case to a lab IR doctor (eg. “There is a septic nephrostomy which I've added. I've checked the bloods etc and everything is good to go. There is space for it this afternoon.”)

Lab IR team

  • Lab IR doctors, nurses and radiographers should avoid engaging in other referral or admin-related queries, even in an effort to be helpful.
  • Clinical questions about patients due to have or having already had procedures (eg. “Do you want to review this groin haematoma?”) can be directed to the relevant lab IR (ie. the one who did the procedure) if they are available but the majority of these queries will likely go to the duty team. Some judgement will be required here.
  • For cases consented immediately prior to recovery, every effort should be made to ensure the consenting doctor (or supervising consultant for registrar consent or cases) performs the procedure.
  • On the other hand, lab IR doctors should not consent multiple recovery patients at the same time if they know they are unlikely to be performing the procedure, even if trying to be helpful.
  • Lab IR doctors, nurses and radiographers should refrain from asking too many questions about cases going through duty and concentrate on the case in front of them.

Emergency cases requiring a third practitioner

  • If two scheduled cases are happening and a third QEUH case needs to be performed as an emergency, duty IR should perform the case (even if it is more interesting than the lab IR cases and the lab IR doctor would really quite like to do it…)
  • The duty phone should go to the duty nurse or radiographer: if another emergency occurs, a doctor currently performing a procedure can be interrupted (this should be rare.)

Emergency cases requiring IR to travel to another hospital

  • Duty IR doctor is expected to travel.
  • GGH and GRI have radiographers so the duty IR radiographer is likely to stay in QEUH.
  • Depending on nursing staff (and potentially on-call staff if the case is near 5pm) the duty IR nurse may or may not have to travel.
  • The phone should stay in QEUH with either the duty IR nurse or radiographer.
  • However, as above, duty IR should remain duty IR: calls requiring discussion can be forwarded to theatre K or GGH or messages taken.
  • Clinicians who visit in person should be either asked to come back, phone the hospital where duty IR doctor is or wait until there might be an available lab IR doctor, though this should be discouraged for routine discussions.

Covering absences

  • Duty IR doctor should be filled in preference to lab IR doctor, which means lists will have to be closed.
  • This is in the interest of patient safety but may impact on waiting times.

Side-of-the-board

  • The side-of-the-board contains patient identifiable information and is freely visible to any visitors to the department (other clinicians, industry representatives, Toshiba engineers, interpreters and other frequent visitors.)
  • Its use to record case details is probably better avoided.
  • As detailed above, it would be reasonable to use it to record cases where patients do not yet have a CRIS entry and cases which may need handed on to the on-call team.

Hand-over

  • This should be formalised.
  • The on-call doctor should phone duty IR doctor to handover any cases and what action is required.
  • If there is nothing to handover, a text message or phone call stating so is preferable to no handover.
  • The duty IR doctor should phone the on-call doctor around 5pm to handover pending or possible cases currently on the side-of-the-board.
  • Ideally, with this handover performed, the board should be wiped clean.
  • If any of these cases need to be handed back over to next day's team, it is the responsibility of the on-call team.

Summary

The suggestions above are intended to improve the safety of patients visiting the busiest IR site in Glasgow.

Ideal side effects will be improved staff morale as lab teams should be able to focus on performing scheduled care to the highest standard.

Unscheduled care inherently carries more risks, but with a formal structure in place to deal with it, this can be minimised.

A negative side-effect is that this will likely have something of a negative impact on the amount of work performed - a list should be closed in preference to a duty slot being vacant.

While this may seem unacceptable, consider the anaesthetic team: they prioritise patient (and team) safety above all else. This often leads to delays, which the anaesthetic team accept better than anyone else. However, when the anaesthetic team are fully supporting an IR case, everyone feels like they are in safer hands.

We should strive to cultivate this atmosphere in IR.
